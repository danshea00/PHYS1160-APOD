Jupiter

Jupiter is the largest planet in our solar system. One of the four Jovian planets in our solar system, the gas giant is named after the Roman king of gods, Jupiter. The planet’s observation was first recorded by Babylonian astronomers as early as the 8th century BC (Sachs, 1974). To the Babylonians, the planet represented the god Marduk (Rogers, 1998). Jupiter’s orbital period of just under 12 years is the basis for both the Babylonian and Chinese zodiacs (Dubs, 1958). Since these early observations, and with the technological advancements that have allowed for space exploration, we have gained a much clearer picture of Jupiter.

Jupiter has a semi-major axis of approximately 5.2 AU and an eccentricity of 0.0489. With a volume of 143128e10 km^3, Jupiter is 1321 times larger than Earth, despite being only 318 times more massive. The planet is composed of mostly hydrogen and helium, with a composition of 89% and 10% by volume respectively (NASA, 2018). This proportion of hydrogen and helium is close to the composition of the primordial solar nebula. Jupiter is often referred to as a “failed star” as it has the right composition to be a star, but is not massive enough to fuse hydrogen into helium.

Jupiter's gravity is shaped by its rotation and atmospheric movements. Measurements from Juno reveal a north-south gravitational asymmetry, pointing to internal and atmospheric activity (Iess et al, 2021).

Discovered by Galileo, the planet has four moons: Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto. The largest, Ganymede, is even larger than Mercury, and the smallest, Europa, is about as large as Earth’s moon. Io, Europa, and Ganymede orbit according to the Laplace resonance, with their orbital periods exhibiting a ratio of 4:2:1 respectively.

Jupiter’s Atmosphere

The atmosphere of Jupiter is very well understood after the Galileo atmospheric probe descended into the gas giant in 1995, measuring the temperature, pressure, gas composition, clouds, radiant energy, and lightning.

The main components of Jupiter’s atmosphere are molecular hydrogen and helium. The atmosphere also contains simple compounds such as water, ammonia, and hydrogen sulphide, as well as hydrocarbons as heavy as Benzene (Atreya, 2003). The atmosphere is divided into four layers: the troposphere, the stratosphere, the thermosphere, and the exosphere. Due to the high temperatures and pressures at the lowest layer of the atmosphere, there is a smooth transition between Jupiter’s liquid centre and the troposphere. The base of this layer is considered to be approximately 90 km below the planets ‘surface’ of 1 bar, where the pressure reaches 10 bar (Seiff, 1998). The layer extends to 50 km beyond the planet’s surface, where temperatures decrease from 340 K to 110 K. The troposphere also contains cloud structures made of ammonia ice (Baines, 2002). It is these structures that create the red and white bands of Jupiter which can be seen from earth. The stratosphere extends beyond the tropopause, rising to an altitude of 320 km where pressures are 1 μbar. In this layer, temperatures increase to about 200 K at the transition to the thermosphere. The stratosphere hosts haze layers composed of condensed heavy hydrocarbons or hydrazine, generated from methane in the upper stratosphere. In the thermosphere, which extends beyond 1000 km, temperatures further rise to 800–1000 K. Notable phenomena in the thermosphere include airglow, polar aurorae, and X-ray emissions (Yelle and Miller, 2004). Layers of increased electron and ion density form the ionosphere within the thermosphere. The exosphere, located beyond approximately 5000 km above the surface, sees a gradual decrease in density. This outermost layer emits X-rays at the poles and low latitudes, with permanent aurorae encircling the poles (Bhardwaj and Gladstone, 2000). Jupiter's atmosphere exhibits a complex and dynamic structure across the various layers.

Jupiter's atmosphere, primarily composed of molecular hydrogen and helium, exhibits a stratified structure with distinct layers, from the troposphere characterised by ammonia ice clouds to the thermosphere displaying phenomena such as airglow, polar aurorae, and X-ray emissions.



The Great Red Spot

Jupiter’s Great Red Spot is the largest anticyclonic storm in our solar system. The storm rotates anticlockwise and is located 22 degrees south of the equator. The Great Red Spot has existed since at least the 19th century, though it may have formed as early as 1665, however these observations could have been of a different storm. Figure X shows Donato Creti’s impression of a stormy Jupiter, painted in 1711. As detailed records exist since 1879, the size and drift of The Great Red Spot can be measured over many years (Simon, 2018). Anticyclones, with high pressure central regions, spin the opposite way to conventional cyclones, which have low pressure central regions. The stable nature of anticyclones in combination with the size, depth, and vorticity of The Great Red Spot, allows for the storm’s longevity (Sánchez‐Lavega, 2021).

Analysis of the storm using microwave radiometry data from the Juno mission indicated that it is 200 to 500 km deep, extending to a base of 240 km below the surface where pressures reach 100 bar (Parisi et al, 2018). A more recent study determined that The Great Red Spot is more deeply rooted, with a depth of greater than 500 km (Cai et al, 2023). The storm’s colour has also changed over time, becoming more intensely orange over time. This is possibly correlated with a phenomenon known as ‘vortex stretching’ (Simon, 2018).

The Great Red Spot has been observed to be shrinking in length since the 1800s. Since 1987, when its length was 40,000 km, the storm has shrunk to a length of 15,000 km today (Sánchez‐Lavega, 2021). It currently continues to shrink at 0.194°/yr; a rate which will see the storm transition from oval to circular by 2040. The storm travels westward, at a rate that has increased since the 1980’s. Since 2014, the Great Red Spot has exhibited a diminishing reflectance in the short wavelength range (< 650 nm), accompanied by an increase in brightness at 890 nm. These changes suggest alterations in the composition of clouds or haze at elevated altitudes (Simon, 2018). From 2018 to 2020, the spot began to ‘flake’, with fragments dissipating at the edges of the storm. While there was speculation that the storm was beginning to dissipate, new research claims that these events were superficial, not affecting the entire depth of The Great Red Spot (Sánchez‐Lavega, 2021).

Researchers have simulated stable 3-dimensional vortices within Jupiter's atmosphere. Two types were explored: "constant-vorticity," which are similar to Jupiter's Great Red Spot, and "constant-area" vortices. The simulation indicated stable vortices resembling the Great Red Spot emerged from the constant-vorticity type, while constant-area vortices proved unstable. Late-stage vortices exhibited unexpected features, including hollowing akin to the Great Red Spot and alignment with Jupiter's spin axis (Zhang, 2023).

Jupiter's Great Red Spot has endured for centuries and presents a unique subject of study. Detailed observational records have allowed for a comprehensive analysis of its size, drift, and evolving characteristics. Recent research indicates a substantial depth, surpassing 500 km. The storm's colour transformation and the observed shrinking since the 1800s, coupled with altered reflectance and 'flaking' events, highlight the dynamic nature of the storm. Simulations of 3-dimensional vortices offer valuable insights into the stability and unexpected features of structures resembling the Great Red Spot within Jupiter's atmosphere.

The first mission, Pioneer 10, studied Jupiter’s atmosphere and took the first close-up images of Jupiter and its moons. During this flyby mission, the spacecraft determined many properties including the rough composition of the planet and the fact that the magnetic field is the opposite to that of the Earth. The mission was the first spacecraft to escape the solar system.

Voyager 1, the furthest man made object from earth, performed a gravity assist around Jupiter, gaining 10 km/s. During the manoeuvre, it captured close-up images of the atmosphere of Jupiter, including the APOD from the 2nd of May 2011.

The most recent mission, Juno, entered a polar orbit of Jupiter on the 5th of July, 2016. It studied the planet's gravitation and magnetism, as well as its winds and atmosphere.

One upcoming ESA mission is Jupiter Icy Moons Explorer (JUICE). Its instruments include the Ganymede Laser Altimeter (GALA), which utilises a laser to study the topography of icy moons and tidal deformations of Ganymede and the Sub-millimeter Wave Instrument (SWI), which utilizes a spectrometer to study Jupiter's stratosphere and troposphere, as well as the exospheres and surfaces of the icy moons.
