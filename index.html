Jupiter's Atmosphere

The atmosphere of Jupiter is very well understood after the Galileo atmospheric probe descended into the gas giant in 1995, measuring the temperature, pressure, gas composition, clouds, radiant energy, and lightning. The main components of Jupiter’s atmosphere are molecular hydrogen and helium. The atmosphere also contains simple compounds such as water, ammonia, and hydrogen sulphide, as well as hydrocarbons as heavy as Benzene (Atreya, 2003). The atmosphere is divided into four layers: the troposphere, the stratosphere, the thermosphere, and the exosphere. Due to the high temperatures and pressures at the lowest layer of the atmosphere, there is a smooth transition between Jupiter’s liquid centre and the troposphere. The base of this layer is considered to be approximately 90 km below the planets ‘surface’ of 1 bar, where the pressure reaches 10 bar (Seiff, 1998). The layer extends to 50 km beyond the planet’s surface, where temperatures decrease from 340 K to 110 K. The troposphere also contains cloud structures made of ammonia ice (Baines, 2002). It is these structures that create the red and white bands of Jupiter which can be seen from earth. The stratosphere extends beyond the tropopause, rising to an altitude of 320 km where pressures are 1 μbar. In this layer, temperatures increase to about 200 K at the transition to the thermosphere. The stratosphere hosts haze layers composed of condensed heavy hydrocarbons or hydrazine, generated from methane in the upper stratosphere. In the thermosphere, which extends beyond 1000 km, temperatures further rise to 800–1000 K. Notable phenomena in the thermosphere include airglow, polar aurorae, and X-ray emissions (Yelle and Miller, 2004). Layers of increased electron and ion density form the ionosphere within the thermosphere. The exosphere, located beyond approximately 5000 km above the surface, sees a gradual decrease in density. This outermost layer emits X-rays at the poles and low latitudes, with permanent aurorae encircling the poles (Bhardwaj and Gladstone, 2000). Jupiter's atmosphere exhibits a complex and dynamic structure across the various layers. In conclusion, Jupiter's atmosphere, primarily composed of molecular hydrogen and helium, exhibits a stratified structure with distinct layers, from the troposphere characterised by ammonia ice clouds to the thermosphere displaying phenomena such as airglow, polar aurorae, and X-ray emissions.
